The short version

Your advertising is profitable — for every €1 spent you get €7.41 back, which is a healthy return. The fundamentals are solid. But this audit found three things that are costing you money every day: €4,700/month going to ads that produce zero sales, a high-converting ad position (the top of search results) that's barely being used, and over €88,000 in sales coming from competitor product targeting that's happening by accident — not by design. There's meaningful money being left on the table, and none of it requires starting from scratch.

What's working well

Before getting into problems, it's worth being clear about what the account does well. These are real strengths, not just diplomatic padding.

The auto-campaigns are carrying the account

Five broad campaigns (A15, A20, A10, A07, A04) act as catch-all nets across the catalog. They're not optimally structured — we'll come to that — but they're profitable, returning 10x to 49x on ad spend. A15 alone generated €17,207 in sales on €1,288 of spend over 30 days. That's 13.36x ROAS. These campaigns are doing discovery work that most accounts pay significant management fees to run manually.

Your ads are spending efficiently overall

A 12.7% ACoS (advertising cost as a percentage of ad sales) is within the healthy range for most furniture and garden categories on Amazon DE. The account isn't over-spending to generate sales — it's just not maximizing the potential of what it's already doing.

You're winning on competitor products — accidentally

When Amazon shows your ad to someone browsing a competitor's product, that's called substitutes targeting. Your auto-campaigns have been picking this up automatically, and the numbers are striking: over 64 days, €7,711 spent on competitor product pages returned €88,640 in sales — an 11.5x return, significantly higher than your overall average. This is one of the most valuable mechanics in Amazon advertising, and it's happening in your account without anyone specifically setting it up.

Why is this happening?

These search terms are being bid on — in some cases very aggressively — without anyone checking whether they actually convert. "Pergola 3x4" received 946 clicks and €215 of spend without a single sale. Either shoppers searching this term are looking for something different than what's being advertised, or the price point doesn't match their expectations. Either way, the fix is the same: stop bidding on these terms.

The fact that 455 terms with 50+ clicks have zero sales suggests this review hasn't been done systematically. Catching and blocking these terms is called negative keyword management — it's routine maintenance in any actively managed account.

Ads at the top of search results return €19.93 for every €1 spent. Ads on product pages return €6.97. This isn't a product problem. The ads that do appear at the top of search are clearly converting well. The issue is that the account settings are preventing more ads from appearing there.

Problem 4: Hundreds of products sharing the same ad, with no way to tell which ones work

Your main ad campaigns group large numbers of products together. The largest single ad group contains 527 individual products all running under one set of rules, one budget, and one bid price. This is a structural problem that limits visibility and control:

  • When a search term converts well in that group, you can't tell which of the 527 products it converted for — so you can't scale what's working.
  • When a search term wastes money, you can't safely block it without risking cutting off a product that might actually convert on that term.
  • One bid price covers 527 products with different prices and profit margins — some are being overbid, some underbid, and there's no way to know which.

The campaigns are profitable despite this — which shows how strong the underlying catalog is. But the structure is a ceiling on how efficient the account can become. Standard practice for an account this size is to give each product its own campaign so performance can be measured and managed at the individual product level.

The biggest untapped opportunity: turning accidental competitor wins into a system

Earlier we noted that €7,711 spent on competitor product pages returned €88,640 in sales — an 11.5x return happening through the auto-campaigns without anyone deliberately setting it up. This is the highest-returning mechanic in your account, and it's running on luck.

What this means in practice

When someone is looking at a competitor's product on Amazon, your ad can appear there. If the competitor's product is similar to yours but priced higher, or has worse reviews, shoppers are likely to switch — which is why this targeting converts so well. The auto-campaigns are stumbling onto the right competitor products by chance. Building this out deliberately — identifying the exact competitor products where your ads work best and targeting them specifically — would compound an already-strong result.

Transparent note on this estimate: We can't predict exactly how much incremental revenue deliberate competitor targeting would generate — that depends on bid levels, which competitors are targeted, and whether the patterns hold at higher volume. What we can say is that the existing data shows this mechanic works significantly better than average in your account, and it's currently running without any active management.

Summary of findings

  • What's working: Your auto-campaigns are profitable (10–49x returns), your overall advertising efficiency is healthy (€7.41 back per €1 spent), and your ads are already winning on competitor products at 11.5x return — the building blocks are there.
  • €4,700/month in identifiable waste: 455 search terms with 50+ clicks and zero sales. This is recoverable without changing anything else in the account — just blocking these terms from triggering ads.
  • The best ad position generates 3x the return of the worst (19.93x vs 6.97x) but receives only 0.8% of impressions. Account settings are preventing ads from appearing where they perform best.
  • Sponsored Brands underperforms category benchmarks (3.74x vs 5–7x typical) and Sponsored Display is nearly inactive. Both formats exist in the account but aren't being actively managed.
  • Five high-performing products generating €52,455 in 60-day revenue are receiving €484 in ad support. The Recliner Armchair alone returns €35.50 per €1 spent — with €58/month in ad investment.
  • €88,640 in competitor targeting sales is happening by accident through auto-campaigns. Building this out deliberately is the highest-potential opportunity identified in the account.
  • 5,388 products (72.5% of the catalog) have zero sales in 60 days, and there are no campaigns designed to improve their search visibility.
